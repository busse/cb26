The front of this building faces a street in Savannah's historic district. The ironwork is restored. The paint is correct. The signage has been reviewed. Everything visible from the sidewalk has been considered.

This is the back.

Six electric meters are bolted to the wall in a steel panel, each one tracking a separate tenant's draw. Junction boxes flank the door — three of them on the stucco side, mounted at different heights from different eras, their covers tagged with marker. PVC conduit runs vertically from the meter panel into the building's guts, three pipes climbing the brick in parallel, clean and white against century-old mortar. The conduit is newer than everything around it. It will outlast the businesses it serves.

The door tells its own story. Security bars over translucent glass, a deadbolt, the kind of entrance that exists to be locked. This is a door that has never welcomed anyone. It admits deliveries, contractors, and the occasional employee who needs air. The graffiti crowd knows this: the door frame is tagged, the meter panel is tagged, the junction boxes are tagged, the adjacent wall is tagged. Every surface in this alley that isn't actively maintained has been claimed by the fastest layer.

What interests me is the stratigraphy. The brick on the right is original — probably mid-nineteenth century, mortar joints receding, each brick a different shade of fired clay. The stucco on the left is a later application, covering whatever the original face looked like, now itself peeling and stained. The conduit is recent, maybe a decade old. The meters are current-generation digital units with RF collars for remote reading. And the graffiti is the newest thing here — days or weeks old, applied by hands that will never touch the wiring or the meters or the mortar.

Each layer operates at its own tempo. The brick hasn't changed in a century and a half. The stucco is on a fifty-year cycle. The meters get swapped every decade or two. The graffiti turns over in months. Brand's model isn't theoretical here — it's literally stacked on this wall, each pace layer physically adjacent to the others, each one indifferent to the rest.

The alley is where the building drops its performance. The street-facing facade is culture — curated, restored, governed by a historic district review board. The alley is infrastructure — functional, unreviewed, honest about what the building actually needs to operate. Every city has this split, but Savannah makes it especially legible because the front is so carefully maintained. The distance between the two faces is about thirty feet and an entire philosophy of what a building is for.
