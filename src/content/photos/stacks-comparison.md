Twenty-three years between these two frames. Same stacks.

The first image is from February 2003 — shot from inside the industrial yard, surrounded by the working mess of the complex. Concrete tanks curve at the edge of the frame. Snow covers the scatter of pipes, metal scrap, and equipment bins. The elevator towers and the brick smokestack rise out of all of it, embedded in process, part of the system. In 2003 this was a place that *did* something. You can feel the operational weight.

The second image is from March 2026. The vantage point has moved to the street. A brick warehouse fills the foreground — solid, horizontal, a different era's answer to the same economic question. The same towers emerge above it against open sky. Power lines cross the frame. A transformer hangs on its pole. The scale has shifted entirely, from intimate clutter to civic backdrop.

Twenty-three years is enough time for everything in Brand's upper layers to turn over completely. The commerce that justified these stacks in 2003 may have moved, contracted, or reinvented itself. The fashion of what Richmond considers interesting real estate has cycled multiple times. The cultural conversation about industrial heritage has matured from afterthought to active debate. But the stacks are still there. They haven't moved. They haven't adapted. They haven't needed to. That's what infrastructure does — it absorbs time.

What strikes me most is how completely the context rewrites the subject without touching it. In the 2003 frame, surrounded by equipment and snow, the stacks feel *working* — functional, unsentimental, doing their job. In the 2026 frame, backed off to the street behind that warehouse wall, they feel *monumental* — artifacts that have outlasted the world that built them. The concrete is identical. The reading is entirely different.

The brick warehouse in the 2026 frame is worth its own meditation. It represents a different generation of infrastructure — human-scaled, horizontal, probably older than the elevators. The two structures don't compete so much as narrate different chapters of the same economy. Brick gave way to poured concrete. Warehousing gave way to vertical processing. But both persist, side by side, because infrastructure is too expensive to remove without reason and too durable to decay on a human timescale.

The nature layer, cycling through twenty-three winters and summers between these frames, has done exactly nothing to the structure. Snow, rain, sun, heat — the stacks absorb all of it. That's the point. Infrastructure operates at a pace so slow it looks like permanence. It isn't permanent. But it's slow enough to make everything else look like weather.
