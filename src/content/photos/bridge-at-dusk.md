There is a particular quality of light in Richmond at dusk where the James River bridges stop being functional and start being compositional. The steel and concrete become lines against the sky — gestures that were engineered, not drawn, but read the same way.

This bridge was built for an economy that no longer exists in its original form. The tobacco warehouses it once connected to rail have become apartments and breweries. The bridge remains, carrying traffic patterns its designers never anticipated, absorbing use cases that would have been unimaginable when the rivets were set.

That is what infrastructure does when it works. It outlasts its original purpose. It becomes a platform for things that haven't been invented yet. The bridge doesn't care whether you're hauling tobacco or commuting to a co-working space. It carries the load either way.

The river, of course, was there before the bridge and will be there after it. Nature sets the constraint. Infrastructure responds to it. Everything else — the commerce, the culture, the fashion of what we do on either side — moves faster and changes more often. But the bridge and the river remain.
