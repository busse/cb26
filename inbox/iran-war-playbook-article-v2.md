## Abstract

This article conducts a systematic comparison between the pre-2026 strategic planning literature on US military options against Iran and the operational sequence of Operation Epic Fury as it has unfolded through March 14, 2026. Drawing on the Brookings Institution's *Which Path to Persia?* (2009), Carter-era National Security Council records, CSIS scenario analyses, investigative reporting by Seymour Hersh, and academic debates in *Foreign Affairs*, the article identifies striking structural correspondences between planning documents and the observable campaign. The operation appears to simultaneously execute at least four of the nine options the Brookings report analyzed as discrete alternatives, deploys the GBU-57 Massive Ordnance Penetrator exactly as purpose-built against Fordow, and activates Kharg Island targeting concepts first proposed in 1979. Yet the war has also, thus far, validated the critics: the Strait of Hormuz insurance-driven shutdown, the unresolved nuclear stockpile, and the apparent failure of decapitation to produce regime collapse each track warnings embedded in the very documents the campaign appears to follow. The conflict remains in progress, and the analysis offered here is necessarily provisional.

**Keywords:** *Iran, Operation Epic Fury, nuclear proliferation, strategic planning, Strait of Hormuz, Kharg Island, bunker-buster munitions, special operations, preventive war*

## I. Introduction

Operation Epic Fury, launched on February 28, 2026, represents what appears to be the culmination of strategic planning that stretches back to the Carter administration. The joint US-Israeli campaign against Iran—which, as of this writing, has struck over 15,000 targets across 26 of Iran's 31 provinces in its first fifteen days[^1]—did not emerge from improvisation. It has activated a strategic architecture assembled across four decades, documented in publicly available planning frameworks, investigative journalism, think-tank analyses, and academic debates. This article asks a simple question: to what extent does the observable campaign follow a pre-existing playbook?

The answer, even at this early stage, is complex. The war's offensive architecture maps onto prior planning documents with remarkable fidelity. Yet the campaign's consequences so far—the Strait of Hormuz closure, the unresolved nuclear stockpile, the failure of leadership decapitation to produce regime collapse—equally validate the warnings those same documents contained. What has emerged to date is a war that follows the playbook on offense while confronting the very retaliation dynamics it was warned to expect.

It bears emphasis that any assessment written on Day 15 of an active conflict carries inherent limitations. The operational picture is incomplete. Intelligence assessments may be revised. Iran's strategic responses are still developing. The analysis that follows should be read as a first-order comparison of planning against observable action, not as a definitive history. Subsequent events may confirm, complicate, or overturn the patterns identified here.

## II. The Brookings Framework: Four Options Executed Simultaneously

The foundational planning document for understanding Operation Epic Fury is Kenneth Pollack and colleagues' *Which Path to Persia? Options for a New American Strategy toward Iran*, published by the Brookings Institution in June 2009.[^2] The study systematically analyzed nine policy options organized into four categories: diplomacy and engagement, military options (airstrikes, a full invasion, and enabling an Israeli strike), regime change, and containment. Each was presented as a discrete alternative, with the authors explicitly cautioning against conflation.

What has materialized in 2026 appears to merge at least four of these options into a single operation—something the report never modeled and implicitly warned against.

Chapter Four's "Osiraq Option" envisioned limited airstrikes on nuclear facilities analogous to Israel's 1981 destruction of Iraq's reactor—a bounded campaign designed to delay Iran's nuclear timeline without triggering broader conflict.[^3] Operation Epic Fury's opening salvo of approximately 2,000 targets in the first twenty-four hours—nearly double the scale of Iraq's 2003 "shock and awe" campaign, according to CENTCOM's Admiral Brad Cooper[^4]—bears little resemblance to a surgical Osiraq-style strike. Whatever this campaign is, it has already exceeded the Osiraq model by an order of magnitude.

Chapter Five analyzed a scenario in which Israel would strike first, providing the United States with plausible deniability.[^5] The actual sequencing partially mirrors this architecture: Israel's June 2025 "Operation Rising Lion" during the Twelve-Day War struck first, with the United States joining via "Operation Midnight Hammer" nine days later. By February 2026, however, the pretense of separation had collapsed. Operations Epic Fury and Roaring Lion launched simultaneously, with over 200 Israeli fighter jets flying the largest combat sortie in Israeli history alongside American Tomahawks, F-22s, and F-15E Strike Eagles. The Brookings report had warned that even an Israeli-only strike would be attributed to Washington; the 2026 reality dispensed with the fiction entirely.

Chapter Six's regime change option appears to have been activated in practice if not in explicit doctrine.[^6] Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei was reported killed in the February 28 opening strike on the Leadership House compound in Tehran, along with his daughter, son-in-law, grandchild, Defense Minister Aziz Nasirzadeh, IRGC Commander Pakpour, and over forty senior officials. The Brookings report had analyzed regime change as requiring sustained ground forces and extended commitment. The current campaign appears to attempt it through decapitation from the air—a hybrid approach the original framework did not model.[^7] Whether decapitation can substitute for the sustained investment the Brookings authors considered necessary remains one of the central open questions of the conflict.

Pollack himself, appearing on Bloomberg and Channel 4 in early March 2026, offered a characteristically measured assessment, noting that "it's difficult to change a regime from the air alone"—restating his own 2009 conclusions while watching his framework being stress-tested in real time.

## III. The Strait of Hormuz: The Predicted Catastrophe

Every major pre-war planning document warned that Iran would attempt to close the Strait of Hormuz in retaliation for military strikes. The Brookings report devoted extensive analysis to this risk. Caitlin Talmadge's influential 2008 article in *International Security* concluded that Iran could impede strait traffic for a month or more, imposing severe economic and military costs.[^8] Clayton Seigle's February 18, 2026, CSIS analysis—published ten days before the war began—mapped four escalation scenarios and specifically warned that an insurance-driven shipping halt could close the strait even without a physical blockade.[^9]

Seigle's Scenario Four has, to date, proved the most immediately prescient. By March 2, leading Protection and Indemnity clubs—including Gard, Skuld, NorthStandard, and the London P&I Club—cancelled war risk coverage for Iranian waters with seventy-two-hour notice.[^10] When coverage expired on March 5, commercial traffic through the strait fell to effectively zero. Lloyd's List reported bluntly that the Strait of Hormuz had been effectively closed not by Iran but by the shipping industry itself.[^11]

The mechanism has not been the conventional naval blockade envisioned in decades of war-planning exercises. Iran attacked sixteen or more commercial vessels using inexpensive drones and missiles, killing at least seven seafarers—enough to render insurance untenable. VLCC freight rates hit an all-time high.[^12] CNN reported on March 12 that the Pentagon and National Security Council "significantly underestimated Iran's willingness to close the Strait of Hormuz," with Energy Department and Treasury analyses reportedly given "secondary consideration" in strike planning.[^13] If confirmed by subsequent reporting, this represents the single most consequential planning failure of the campaign: the risk was documented, mapped, and published—and apparently deprioritized.

Seigle's oil price projections proved directionally correct but conservative. His estimate of a ten-to-twelve-dollar-per-barrel increase for a limited disruption scenario was overtaken by the strait-wide shutdown, which pushed Brent crude from approximately seventy dollars pre-war to above one hundred three dollars by March 13—a thirty-dollar-plus increase that triggered the IEA's largest-ever coordinated strategic reserve release of 400 million barrels.[^14] Whether this disruption represents a temporary dislocation or a structural shift in energy markets will depend on how the Hormuz situation resolves—a question that remains entirely open.

## IV. From Admiral Lyons to March 13: The Kharg Island Thread

The operational connection between Admiral James "Ace" Lyons' 1979 proposal to seize Kharg Island and the March 13, 2026, strike is not merely thematic—it was explicitly revived by name. Lyons, then Director of Political-Military Affairs for the Joint Chiefs, proposed giving Iran twenty-four hours to release the hostages or face the destruction of its oil export capacity. Carter's NSC rejected the plan, with Brzezinski warning that "going after Kharg Island would create a worldwide oil crisis."[^15]

Michael Rubin, an AEI senior fellow and former Pentagon adviser on Iran under Secretary Rumsfeld, published "'Take the Oil': Seizing the Kharg Island Terminal is the Ultimate Checkmate to Iran" on January 13, 2026—six weeks before the war—directly citing Lyons' plan as his template.[^16] Multiple outlets subsequently reported that Rubin's recommendations had been circulated within the Trump administration's National Security Council. His prior piece at AEI had argued that seizure rather than destruction would bankrupt the regime while preserving infrastructure for post-regime-change reconstruction.[^17]

What materialized on March 13 was a partial implementation. CENTCOM executed precision aerial strikes destroying over ninety military targets on Kharg Island—naval mine storage facilities, missile bunkers, air defense systems, the Joshen naval base—while deliberately preserving oil infrastructure.[^18] Trump's Truth Social statement framed the strike as conditional leverage: the oil facilities would be destroyed if Iran continued interfering with Hormuz shipping. The simultaneous deployment of the 31st Marine Expeditionary Unit aboard the USS Tripoli Amphibious Ready Group—approximately 2,200 Marines with F-35Bs, MV-22B Ospreys, and heavy-lift helicopters—kept the physical seizure option credible.[^19]

The progression from Lyons' 1979 desk to Rubin's January 2026 advocacy to Trump's March 13 strike is traceable through an unbroken chain of public documents. What remains unresolved—and what may be the most consequential decision still ahead—is whether the campaign will advance to the seizure phase that Rubin prescribed. The 31st MEU is currently in transit and estimated to arrive in the Gulf region within ten to fifteen days. Its arrival will present the administration with a choice between the risks of amphibious seizure fifteen miles from the Iranian mainland and the costs of a prolonged Hormuz closure. That decision has not yet been made, and this analysis cannot predict its outcome.

## V. The GBU-57 at Fordow and the Unresolved Nuclear Stockpile

The GBU-57A/B Massive Ordnance Penetrator saw its first combat use during Operation Midnight Hammer on June 22, 2025. Seven B-2 Spirit stealth bombers departed Whiteman Air Force Base, flew eighteen-plus hours with three mid-air refueling stops, and dropped fourteen GBU-57s: twelve on Fordow and two on Natanz.[^20] The weapon was purpose-built for this target over a fifteen-year, three-hundred-million-dollar development program. Fordow's enrichment halls sit beneath 260 to 300 feet of rock and concrete. At Fordow, the attack method reportedly exploited the facility's ventilation architecture: the first bomb removed the shaft cap, and subsequent weapons entered at over 1,000 feet per second to detonate inside the underground complex.

The Air Force subsequently awarded Boeing a sixty-one-million-dollar sole-source contract to replenish GBU-57 inventory, described as the final production lot before transition to a Next Generation Penetrator sized for the B-21 Raider.[^21] In the February–March 2026 campaign, GBU-57s appear to have been used again against the Taleghan 2 facility at Parchin, where satellite imagery showed three large impact craters consistent with MOP strikes—though CENTCOM has not confirmed this.

Yet the weapon's tactical success has not, as of this writing, resolved the strategic problem. Iran held approximately 450 kilograms of sixty-percent-enriched uranium—sufficient, with further enrichment, for roughly eleven nuclear weapons. The June 2025 strikes reportedly buried much of this material under rubble but did not destroy or remove it. By March 2026, intelligence suggested Iran had regained partial access to uranium at Isfahan. Secretary of State Rubio told Congress: "People are going to have to go and get it."[^22]

This is precisely the scenario for which the Nuclear Disablement Teams (NDTs) trained. In 2023–2024, NDT personnel partnered with the 75th Ranger Regiment and 5th Special Forces Group in exercises simulating hostile seizure of underground nuclear facilities.[^23] Semafor reported on March 7, 2026, that CENTCOM had developed contingency plans for commando raids on Isfahan, Fordow, and Qom.[^24] As of this writing, however, no ground operation has been publicly confirmed. When Putin offered to again accept Iran's uranium—reprising Russia's role in the 2015 JCPOA shipment[^25]—Trump rejected the proposal.[^26] The war continues while the diplomatic mechanism that once achieved the same objective sits unused.[^27] Whether the administration will ultimately order a SOF raid, accept a diplomatic off-ramp, or settle for the ambiguity of partially buried material remains one of the conflict's defining unresolved questions.

## VI. The Kroenig-Kahl Debate: An Interim Verdict

The most prominent intellectual framework for evaluating the military option was the Kroenig-Kahl exchange in *Foreign Affairs*. Matthew Kroenig's January 2012 article "Time to Attack Iran" argued that surgical US strikes could halt the nuclear program for five or more years and that Iran's retaliation would be manageable.[^28] Colin Kahl's rebuttal, "Not Time to Attack Iran," accused Kroenig of pairing "the alarmist worst case of the Iranian nuclear threat with the rosiest best case of America's ability to make things better."[^29]

Fifteen days into Operation Epic Fury, both analysts find partial vindication—though the balance may shift as events develop. Kroenig, debating Trita Parsi in *Foreign Policy* on March 12, maintained that the strikes had "already made the Middle East safer."[^30] The nuclear infrastructure has unquestionably been degraded. But Kroenig's core premise—that retaliation would be absorbable—has been challenged by the Hormuz closure, oil prices exceeding one hundred dollars per barrel, attacks on nine countries including NATO ally Turkey, and thirteen US service member fatalities. Whether these costs prove temporary or structural will determine which analyst history ultimately validates.

Kahl's March 10 *Foreign Affairs* article identified what may be the campaign's deepest structural problem: the administration appears to be pursuing at least four contradictory objectives—regime change, unconditional surrender, nuclear disarmament, and military degradation—each requiring "fundamentally different wars, resources, timelines, and post-conflict plans."[^31] His cost-exchange observation warrants close attention as the conflict continues: Iran possesses an inexhaustible supply of short-range drones costing thirty-five thousand dollars apiece, while the United States intercepts them with two-million-dollar missiles. If this asymmetry persists, the arithmetic of attrition favors the cheaper combatant. But "if" is doing significant work in that sentence—the conflict may yet evolve in ways that alter the exchange ratio.

## VII. Eisenstadt's Prophecy: The Multi-Round Campaign

Michael Eisenstadt of the Washington Institute for Near East Policy stands as perhaps the single most validated analyst to date—though that assessment, like all others here, is provisional. His March 2025 paper, "Attacking Iran's Nuclear Program: The Complex Calculus of Preventive Action," warned that "a preventive attack is unlikely to be a solitary event, but rather the opening round of a long campaign employing military strikes, covert action, as well as economic, informational, and other elements of national power."[^32]

The actual sequence thus far—Israeli air defense degradation (April and October 2024), the Twelve-Day War (June 2025), the interim period of failed diplomacy and protests (July 2025–February 2026), and now Operation Epic Fury (February–March 2026)—tracks precisely the multi-round campaign Eisenstadt described. His earlier October 2023 *War on the Rocks* article had warned that "hardening, burying, and dispersal may eventually put the Islamic Republic's nuclear program beyond the reach of conventional weapons."[^33] In a March 2026 Washington Institute analysis, Eisenstadt characterized the ongoing conflict as "a race to degrade Iranian launch capabilities faster than they can be replenished."[^34] Whether that race has been won—or whether the concept of "winning" such a race is coherent—remains to be seen.

## VIII. Hersh's Infrastructure and the Reconnaissance Foundation

Seymour Hersh's reporting in *The New Yorker* between 2005 and 2008 described an extensive covert infrastructure: JSOC commandos conducting reconnaissance inside Iran from 2004 onward, a four-hundred-million-dollar covert destabilization program authorized by a Bush-era Presidential Finding, and air campaign planning with approximately 400 aim points.[^35] [^36] [^37] The actual campaign has exceeded these reported parameters by an order of magnitude—over 15,000 targets in fifteen days. Yet the foundational intelligence infrastructure Hersh described—JSOC reconnaissance, covert minority group support, comprehensive target databases—plausibly provided the architecture on which the current campaign was constructed. The degree to which 2005-era intelligence pipelines directly fed 2026 targeting packages cannot be confirmed from open sources, but the structural continuity is suggestive.

Hersh published "What I Have Been Told Is Coming in Iran" on his Substack on June 19, 2025, just before the Twelve-Day War, predicting heavy American bombing.[^38] His core thesis—that the United States had been planning a major air campaign against Iran for two decades—appears to have been confirmed by events. His specific timeline predictions were repeatedly premature, but his structural analysis aged as a warning that was dismissed until the day it became operational reality. Future historians will need to assess how much of Hersh's sourcing reflected actual planning and how much reflected the aspirations of his sources.

## IX. The Collapse of the Three-Way Bluff

Bergman and Mazzetti's 2019 *New York Times Magazine* investigation described the Israel-US-Iran dynamic as "a kind of three-way bluff": Israel wanted the world to believe it would strike, the United States wanted Iran to believe America might attack, and Iran wanted everyone to think retaliation would be devastating.[^39] All three parties have now called each other's bluffs. Israel struck (June 2025), the United States struck (June 2025 and February 2026), and Iran retaliated devastatingly (closing Hormuz, striking nine countries, killing American service members). The bluff became reality.

Al Jazeera's March 11 analysis drew a direct line from the Twelve-Day War to the current conflict, arguing that the 2025 "blueprint" had "trapped" the United States and Israel in a longer war.[^40] *War on the Rocks* drew an explicit parallel to 1914's Austria-Hungary/Germany dynamic, arguing the United States had given Israel a "blank check" that transformed posturing into co-belligerency. The Intercept's 2023 revelation of the classified CONPLAN codenamed "Support Sentry"—funded in FY2018 and FY2019—confirms that the infrastructure for this war was built before the political decision to launch it.[^41]

Whether the "trap" metaphor proves apt depends entirely on what happens next. If the Hormuz crisis resolves quickly and Iran's regime fractures, the June 2025 strikes will be reinterpreted as the necessary first round of a successful campaign. If the conflict grinds on and the nuclear stockpile remains unsecured, the "entrapment" narrative will harden. This article cannot adjudicate between these futures. It can only note that the planning documents anticipated both.

## X. Conclusion: The Playbook Predicted the War—Its Trajectory Remains Unwritten

As of Day 15 of Operation Epic Fury, the observable campaign follows pre-existing planning documents with remarkable structural fidelity. The Brookings options framework, the Lyons/Kharg Island concept, the CSIS disruption scenarios, Hersh's covert infrastructure reporting, the GBU-57's purpose-built targeting of Fordow, the Nuclear Disablement Team training, and Eisenstadt's multi-round campaign prediction all map onto the operational reality as it has so far developed. This was not a war conceived in February 2026. It was a war assembled from components designed, funded, trained, and publicly analyzed across four decades.

But the playbooks have, thus far, predicted the war's opening better than its evolution. Three critical gaps between planning and reality have emerged in the first fifteen days—though it is too early to know whether they represent temporary disruptions or structural failures. First, the insurance-driven Hormuz shutdown—identified by CSIS but reportedly not adequately planned for by the Pentagon—has created the worst energy disruption since the 1970s.[^42] Second, the nuclear stockpile remains unsecured despite two rounds of strikes, leaving the war's central stated objective unachieved. Third, the decapitation of Khamenei produced not regime collapse but rapid succession, with Mojtaba Khamenei reportedly installed within eight days and the IRGC maintaining operational capacity.

The JCPOA comparison introduces a painful irony, though one that may yet be rendered moot by events. Under the 2015 nuclear deal, Iran voluntarily shipped over 11,000 kilograms of enriched uranium to Russia, reduced its stockpile by ninety-seven percent, and accepted comprehensive verification—achieving through diplomacy what the current campaign seeks to accomplish by force.[^43] The rejection of Putin's March 2026 offer to reprise Russia's 2015 role[^44] suggests that the administration's objectives may extend beyond nonproliferation to regime transformation—a goal the Brookings report explicitly identified as requiring resources and commitments far beyond what an air campaign can deliver. Whether that assessment proves correct is a question the coming weeks and months will answer.

The most telling document may be The Intercept's 2023 revelation of CONPLAN "Support Sentry."[^45] Its existence as a funded "cost of war item" confirms what the public literature suggests: the infrastructure for this war was built long before the political decision to launch it.

As *TIME* observed on March 13, the war with Iran "could be a long one."[^46] Michael Eisenstadt predicted precisely this. The question now is whether American policymakers will heed the other half of his warning: that the "complex calculus of preventive action" requires not merely the capacity to strike, but a coherent theory of how striking leads to an enduring political outcome.[^47] On that dimension, the playbook remains conspicuously blank.

This analysis will require substantial revision as events develop. The correspondences between planning and action documented here are observable and, in several cases, explicitly acknowledged by the planners themselves. Whether they add up to a coherent strategy or a collision of accumulated contingencies is a judgment that Day 15 is too early to render. What can be said with confidence is that the literature predicted the war. Whether it predicted the peace—or whether anyone planned for one—remains to be seen.
